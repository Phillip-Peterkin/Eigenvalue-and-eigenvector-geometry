Criticality-related measures are widely used to summarize near-critical brain dynamics, but they do not directly describe the geometry of fitted multivariate operators. Here we relate standard criticality measures to eigenvalue- and eigenvector-geometry summaries extracted from sliding-window VAR(1) operators fit under a fixed preprocessing and dimensionality-reduction pipeline. In human intracranial recordings, high-gamma activity was more subcritical than broadband activity ($\sigma_{\mathrm{HG}}=0.9735$ vs.~$\sigma_{\mathrm{BB}}=0.9908$; mixed-effects $p<10^{-8}$). Phase-randomized surrogates constrain the interpretation of sensitivity magnitudes, so sensitivity results are interpreted comparatively. Overall, fitted operator-geometry summaries provide complementary descriptive coordinates for variation across frequency bands, individuals, and brain states in these datasets.

\section*{Significance}
Criticality measures provide a compact summary of neural dynamics, but they leave open what changes in ``distance from criticality'' look like in fitted multivariate operators. Using human iEEG and scalp EEG, we show that simple operator-geometry summaries from sliding-window VAR(1) fits (eigenvalue spacing and eigenvector geometry) covary with criticality-related statistics across individuals and separate propofol sedation and sleep stages in ways not explained by alpha- or delta-power. These state effects persist under shared-subspace estimation, and a secondary SEEG analysis in ds004752 supports stable geometry--dynamics relationships more strongly than task-condition contrasts. Together, operator-geometry summaries add a complementary set of descriptive coordinates for inter-individual and state-dependent variability in these datasets.

\section{Introduction}

Many neural recordings show signatures consistent with dynamics near a critical regime that balances stability and responsiveness. Evidence includes neuronal avalanche statistics, long-range temporal correlations, and complexity measures across species, modalities, and behavioral states \citep{Beggs2003,Shriki2013,Lombardi2023,Srinivasan2024,Xin2025,Hengen2025,GirardiSchappo2021,Hardstone2012,Grubov2025,Mesquita2021,LempelZiv1976,Schartner2015,Li2024,Newman2025,Angiolelli2025}. When arousal changes---for example during anesthesia or sleep---these summaries often shift away from their near-critical baseline \citep{Gervais2023,Maschke2024,Angiolelli2025}.

A growing literature uses criticality-related measures as a descriptive axis for state. Propofol and xenon shift multiple criticality summaries away from a near-critical regime, whereas ketamine can preserve near-critical statistics in some analyses \citep{Maschke2024,Curic2024}. During sleep, avalanche scaling can remain approximately intact across stages even as avalanche density and temporal patterning change \citep{Pereira2023,Huo2024}. These results motivate quantifying ``distance from criticality,'' but they leave open a basic question: what does that distance correspond to in the geometry of fitted multivariate dynamics, and how sensitive is it to preprocessing and estimation?

Linear dynamical-system fits provide a complementary, model-based description of state changes. Under propofol, fitted operators can show systematic eigenvalue-spectrum shifts \citep{Eisen2024,Eisen2026}, and different anesthetics can yield distinguishable spectral signatures \citep{Davila2025,BerjagaBuisan2025}. But eigenvalues alone do not fully characterize non-normal dynamics, where eigenvector geometry can shape transient amplification and numerical sensitivity \citep{Barzon2025,Stroud2024,Troude2025,Horrocks2024,Galgali2023,Jarne2023}.

Here we treat sliding-window VAR(1) operators as local linear summaries and extract operator-geometry statistics as additional descriptive coordinates. We focus on minimum eigenvalue spacing and eigenvector non-orthogonality (via an eigenvector condition number). Because these summaries depend on the estimator and on the preprocessing/dimensionality-reduction pipeline, we interpret them as correlational descriptions rather than mechanistic evidence for neural singularities or mathematically exact ``exceptional points.''

We address three questions in human electrophysiology: (1) Do high-gamma and broadband signals occupy distinct criticality-related regimes within intracranial recordings? (2) Across subjects, do criticality measures covary with fitted operator-geometry summaries? (3) Across propofol anesthesia and natural sleep, do fitted geometry summaries change in ways that are not reducible to conventional alpha/delta power under the same pipeline?

We find that high-gamma is more subcritical than broadband, with dissociations among branching ratio, complexity, and long-range correlations. Across subjects, branching ratio covaries strongly with a composite operator-geometry score. In scalp EEG, eigenvalue-spacing changes distinguish propofol sedation and sleep stages while remaining largely independent of alpha and delta power. These state-related geometry effects persist under shared-subspace estimation and across spacing metrics beyond the single minimum-gap statistic.

\section{Results}

The Results are organized around three questions: band-specific differences in iEEG, cross-subject covariation between criticality and fitted operator geometry, and state effects in propofol anesthesia and sleep.

\subsection{High-gamma and broadband activity occupy different criticality-related regimes}
To test whether criticality-related measures depend on frequency content within the same intracranial recordings, we compared high-gamma (HG) and broadband (BB) activity. Branching ratio showed a clear band separation: HG was more subcritical than BB ($\sigma_{\mathrm{HG}}=0.9735$ vs.~$\sigma_{\mathrm{BB}}=0.9908$; paired $t(17)=-5.74$, $p=8.9\times10^{-6}$). A mixed-effects model confirmed a robust band effect (coefficient $=-0.01727$, $p=9.3\times10^{-9}$).

Other criticality-related summaries dissociated in complementary ways. Lempel--Ziv complexity was higher in HG than BB (mean difference $=0.0572$, $t(17)=5.24$, $p=2.9\times10^{-5}$; mixed-effects $p=1.6\times10^{-7}$), whereas detrended fluctuation analysis (DFA) was higher in BB than HG (mean difference $=-0.2568$, $t(17)=-10.04$, $p=1.1\times10^{-9}$; mixed-effects $p=1.0\times10^{-23}$). The avalanche exponent $\tau$ was lower in HG than BB but did not reach conventional significance at the group level ($t(17)=-1.94$, $p=0.065$; mixed-effects $p=0.052$). Taken together, HG appears more subcritical yet more locally complex, while BB sits closer to criticality with stronger long-range temporal structure \citep{Grubov2025}.

Across subjects, the two bands were coupled but offset: HG branching ratio significantly predicted BB branching ratio in a mixed-effects model (coefficient $=0.212$, $p=5.4\times 10^{-5}$), consistent with shared subject-level factors plus a systematic band shift.

\subsection{Fitted operator geometry aligns with criticality and complexity}
We next asked whether subject-to-subject differences in criticality measures are mirrored in the geometry of fitted operators. Across 18 subjects, branching ratio covaried strongly with a composite operator-geometry score (``ND score,'' combining eigenvalue crowding and eigenvector non-orthogonality; $r=0.860$, $p=4.8\times10^{-6}$). This relationship was stable under leave-one-subject-out resampling (jackknife $r$ range: 0.792--0.889). Branching ratio also correlated with tighter eigenvalue spacing (minimum gap; $r=-0.588$, $p=0.0102$).

Complexity showed the complementary pattern. Lempel--Ziv complexity (LZc) correlated negatively with ND score ($r=-0.684$, $p=0.00174$) and positively with spacing ($r=0.745$, $p=3.9\times10^{-4}$). The avalanche exponent $\tau$ also correlated with ND score ($r=0.526$, $p=0.025$), although more weakly than $\sigma$. These associations place criticality-related and operator-geometry summaries in a shared descriptive space, but they do not identify a unique underlying operator or imply a mathematically exact exceptional point \citep{Bergholtz2021}.

Secondary geometric summaries were directionally consistent. The chirality index correlated positively with $\sigma$ ($r=0.526$, $p=0.025$) and negatively with LZc ($r=-0.570$, $p=0.013$), suggesting that eigenvalue-trajectory features covary with criticality and complexity \citep{RafiUlIslam2024,Ding2024}. By contrast, a secondary spectral-radius--overlap test was not significant ($p=0.542$); because this statistic was not archived in the JSON artifacts, we treat it as non-central support rather than a primary result.

\subsection{Minimum eigenvalue spacing is largely independent of alpha and delta power}
A key interpretive question is whether eigenvalue spacing is simply a proxy for conventional oscillatory power. We tested this using alpha power in the propofol dataset and delta power in the sleep dataset.

In propofol EEG, spacing--alpha correlations were close to zero in both states (mean $r=-0.013$ when sedated; mean $r=0.027$ when awake). The largest absolute correlation across subjects was 0.103, and no subject exceeded $|r|>0.3$. Although the correlation shifted slightly between states (paired $t(19)=-4.85$, $p=1.1\times10^{-4}$, Cohen's $d=-1.09$), the within-state pattern indicates that alpha power does not explain spacing.

In sleep EEG, spacing--delta correlations were likewise negligible (mean $r=-0.001\pm0.013$), and no subject showed $|r|>0.3$. Thus, in these datasets, minimum eigenvalue spacing varies with state while remaining largely orthogonal to alpha and delta power. This qualitative independence also held under shared-subspace estimation and when spacing was summarized with median and low-quantile nearest-neighbor metrics, suggesting that it is not driven solely by the single minimum-gap statistic.

\subsection{Propofol is associated with tighter spacing and a small spectral-radius shift}
We next asked whether propofol anesthesia alters fitted operator geometry in scalp EEG. Spectral radius increased under propofol (0.9980 awake vs.\ 1.0025 sedated; $\Delta=0.0045$; $t(19)=-7.44$, $p=4.8\times10^{-7}$, $d=-1.66$; signs reflect the awake-minus-sedated convention). Because spectral radius estimates near unity can reflect both genuine dynamical changes and finite-sample bias, we interpret this shift alongside spacing and rank summaries.

Propofol was also associated with tighter eigenvalue spacing. Minimum spacing decreased from approximately 0.0122 to 0.0114 (paired $t(19)=3.16$, $p=0.0052$, $d=0.71$), with a similar pooled effect across sedation runs ($p=0.0081$, $d=0.66$). Effective rank changed little and was not significant ($p=0.185$). Eigenvector condition number increased as well ($d=-0.72$, $p=0.004$; 17 of 20 subjects in the same direction), suggesting that the state effect involves eigenvector conditioning in addition to eigenvalue crowding. In this pipeline, propofol therefore reorganizes fitted operator geometry without strong evidence for a rank collapse, extending prior eigenvalue-spectrum results \citep{Eisen2024,Eisen2026}. The spacing effect persisted under shared-subspace PCA and under alternative spacing summaries (median and low-quantile nearest-neighbor metrics; \S2.6).

Finally, we asked whether the propofol-related amplification shift could be explained solely by movement toward instability. Within each subject, we residualized per-window log Kreiss constant with respect to the log spectral-radius gap (pooled across conditions). Residual Kreiss still increased from wake to sedation (Cohen's $d=1.82$, Wilcoxon $p<10^{-5}$; 19 of 20 subjects positive). Among subjects with three sedation runs, Kreiss metrics were elevated relative to wakefulness in each run (all pairwise $d>0.88$, all $p<0.006$), although strict monotonic ordering across runs was uncommon (2 of 14 subjects). Propofol also reduced the fraction of stable windows (0.894 awake vs.\ 0.670 sedated), so stable-window amplification analyses are conditioned on a smaller subset under sedation.

\subsection{Sleep shows a distinct pattern from propofol}
We then asked whether natural sleep shows the same fitted-geometry pattern as propofol sedation.

Eigenvalue spacing was smallest in REM sleep. Awake and N3 did not differ reliably (0.0132 vs.\ 0.0127, $p=0.216$, $d=-0.42$). By contrast, spacing narrowed from N3 to REM (0.0127 vs.\ 0.0111, $p=2.4\times10^{-5}$; FDR significant, $d=-2.51$), and awake also differed from REM ($p=8.6\times10^{-5}$, $d=-2.13$). Thus, in this dataset, REM---not N3---occupies the tightest-spacing regime.

Relative spectral sensitivity followed a different ordering. Awake sensitivity was small and not significant (mean $r=0.021\pm0.088$, $t(9)=0.72$, $p=0.492$). N3 sensitivity was highest (mean $r=0.098\pm0.011$, $t(9)=27.49$, $p<10^{-6}$), and REM sensitivity was intermediate but still significant (mean $r=0.074\pm0.019$, $t(9)=11.44$, $p=10^{-6}$). N3 differed from REM ($t(9)=3.57$, $p=0.006$, $d=1.13$), whereas awake versus REM did not reach conventional significance ($p=0.086$). Together, REM shows the tightest spacing while N3 shows the highest relative sensitivity, indicating that pharmacological and physiological routes to reduced arousal engage fitted operator geometry differently \citep{Pereira2023,Huo2024}. The key REM-related spacing contrasts persisted under shared-subspace PCA and were supported by low-quantile nearest-neighbor spacing metrics (\S2.6).

\subsection{Multi-block sleep robustness analysis}\label{sec:multiblock_sleep}
To test whether sleep results depend on how contiguous blocks are selected, we repeated the state-contrast analyses using the top three contiguous blocks per state per subject (instead of only the single longest block), aggregated within subject, and then tested at the group level. The main REM-related spacing effects (N3 vs.~REM and awake vs.~REM) were consistent with the primary analysis, suggesting that the sleep geometry effects are not driven by a single selected segment. This multi-block analysis also yielded an awake versus N3 spacing difference; because it depends on block aggregation choices and within-subject dependence across blocks, we treat it as a robustness outcome rather than a revised headline result.

\subsection{Secondary analyses: cross-frequency coupling, chirality, and spatial effects}
Several additional analyses were informative but secondary to the primary questions.

Phase--amplitude coupling (PAC) showed a selective relationship to temporal scaling. Across subjects, PAC correlated with the avalanche exponent $\tau$ ($r=0.526$, $p=0.025$) but not with branching ratio ($r=-0.059$, $p=0.82$) or LZc ($r=0.102$, $p=0.69$). PAC also did not differ between task-relevant and irrelevant epochs ($p=0.316$) \citep{Daume2024,Sakakura2023}.

Chirality-related summaries were directionally consistent with the operator-geometry results. The chirality index correlated positively with $\sigma$ and negatively with LZc. Per-subject chirality values are archived in the analysis outputs. As with the other geometric summaries, these associations are descriptive and do not establish chirality as an independent mechanism.

Spatially, the sign reversal in the task-related LZc contrast appeared in four of five deeply sampled subjects. Greater posterior coverage also tended to strengthen the effect ($r=0.338$, $p=0.158$). Given the small sample and heterogeneous coverage, we treat these observations as suggestive. Secondary measures show qualitatively consistent patterns but are not used as primary evidence for the main claims.

\subsection{Robustness analyses constrain interpretation}
We performed robustness checks that bear directly on how the operator-geometry results should be interpreted.

\paragraph{Shared-subspace estimation.} A shared-subspace analysis reduces the concern that state-specific PCA bases drive between-state geometry differences. Instead of fitting PCA separately per state, we pooled all states' CSD-transformed data within each subject, fit PCA once, and projected each state onto the same subspace before VAR estimation. In propofol data, the spectral-radius effect remained (shared-subspace $d=-1.72$ vs.\ per-state $d=-1.66$), and spacing separation slightly strengthened (shared-subspace $d=0.78$ vs.\ per-state $d=0.71$). In sleep data, the key N3-versus-REM spacing difference was only modestly attenuated (shared-subspace $d=-2.39$ vs.\ per-state $d=-2.51$), and the awake-versus-REM contrast also persisted (shared-subspace $d=-2.03$ vs.\ per-state $d=-2.13$). All contrasts that were significant under per-state PCA remained FDR significant under shared-subspace estimation ($q<0.05$).

\paragraph{Alternative spacing summaries.} The state effects were not specific to the single minimum-gap statistic. Using nearest-neighbor spacing summaries, propofol-versus-awake differences remained significant for the median metric ($d=0.78$) and the 10th-percentile metric ($d=0.86$) under shared-subspace estimation (both FDR corrected). In sleep, the 10th-percentile metric confirmed the N3-versus-REM contrast ($d=-2.30$, FDR significant), while the median metric showed significant separations for awake versus N3 ($d=-2.69$) and awake versus REM ($d=-2.65$). A separate multi-block sleep analysis (\S\ref{sec:multiblock_sleep}) also supported the headline REM-related effects; we treat the additional awake-versus-N3 finding as a robustness outcome rather than a revised headline effect.

Phase-randomized surrogates constrain how we interpret sensitivity magnitudes. In a subset of 5 propofol subjects with 200 phase-randomized surrogates each, group sensitivity in real data ($r=0.076$) did not exceed the surrogate mean ($r=0.097$; group $t(4)=-1.41$, $p=0.23$). Increasing surrogate count did not change the conclusion: positive radius--spacing correlations can arise from autoregressive fitting to autocorrelated signals, so within-condition sensitivity magnitude is not interpreted as a standalone neural marker.

The cross-subject coupling between propofol-induced spacing change and sensitivity change was similar after controlling for alpha-power change (raw $r=-0.683$; partial $r=-0.676$).

Relative sensitivity also remained nonzero across PCA dimensionalities. Across 10, 15, and 20 components, group-level sensitivity was reliably above zero (all $p<10^{-6}$), and per-subject values are archived in the analysis outputs. Overall, the primary conclusions rest on (i) band-specific dissociations in criticality-related measures, (ii) cross-subject covariation between branching ratio and ND score, (iii) weak dependence of spacing on alpha/delta power, and (iv) distinct propofol versus sleep patterns in fitted spacing; sensitivity results are reported as comparative summaries under the surrogate constraint.

A secondary transient-amplification analysis further separated which fitted-operator summaries track amplification. In iEEG, fitted operators showed amplification above phase-randomized surrogate baselines in all 18 subjects. Across subjects, Kreiss constants were strongly predicted by eigenvector condition number ($r=0.91$, $p=1.4\times10^{-7}$) but not by ND score ($r=0.058$, $p=0.82$) or branching ratio ($r=0.19$, $p=0.46$). This pattern suggests that amplification in these fitted operators is driven primarily by global eigenvector conditioning rather than by the gap-overlap composite (ND score).

\subsection{Secondary SEEG analysis in ds004752}
We next asked whether any of the geometry--dynamics relationships generalize to an independent depth-electrode dataset. We therefore analyzed SEEG recordings from OpenNeuro ds004752 ($n=15$ subjects; Schweizerische Epilepsie-Klinik, Zurich; Sternberg verbal working memory task) using the same preprocessing and fitted operator-geometry pipeline. Group-level task-condition contrasts (high vs.\ low load) for spacing (gap), ND score, and spectral radius were not significant (all $p>0.2$). By contrast, two within-subject geometry--dynamics relationships were consistent across subjects: spectral sensitivity was positive on average (mean $r\approx0.097$, $p\approx2.5\times10^{-8}$; FDR significant), and effective rank showed a small negative relationship with ND score (mean $r\approx-0.015$, $p\approx6\times10^{-5}$; FDR significant). These results support cross-dataset generalization of specific geometry--dynamics relationships across electrode modalities and paradigms, while also illustrating that task-condition contrasts are not robust in this dataset under the present pipeline.

\section{Discussion}

Sliding-window VAR(1) fits provide a compact way to summarize local multivariate dynamics, but they are rarely discussed in geometric terms. Here we show that simple operator-geometry summaries---derived from fitted linear operators under a fixed preprocessing and dimensionality-reduction pipeline---provide an additional descriptive coordinate system for variation in human electrophysiology. Across datasets, these summaries (i) differ between high-gamma and broadband activity within subjects, (ii) covary with criticality-related measures across subjects, and (iii) separate propofol sedation from wakefulness and distinguish sleep stages. Throughout, these are statements about fitted operators produced by a specific estimator and pipeline, and they should be read as correlational descriptions rather than mechanistic claims.

\subsection{Fitted operator geometry aligns with criticality and complexity}
The most prominent cross-subject relationship links branching ratio to the composite operator-geometry score (ND score; $r=0.86$). Subjects closer to a near-critical regime by avalanche statistics tended to show tighter eigenvalue spacing and stronger eigenvector non-orthogonality in their fitted operators. This pattern is consistent with work showing that eigenvector geometry can shape transient amplification and numerical sensitivity in non-normal systems \citep{Schomerus2024,Kullig2025,Fyodorov2025,Zou2024}. In the present setting, however, it should be interpreted as an estimator-dependent association, not as evidence for a specific underlying mechanism.

\paragraph{Why ``ND score'' is only shorthand} ND score combines two fitted-operator summaries: eigenvalue crowding and eigenvector non-orthogonality. We use it as a compact label that connects to the non-Hermitian linear-systems literature. It does not imply a mathematically exact exceptional point, a topological invariant, or sensing-like phenomena in neural dynamics.

Spacing was also largely independent of alpha (propofol) and delta (sleep) power. This reduces a common confound in state comparisons---that apparent ``dynamical'' effects can be explained by changes in oscillatory power or covariance structure---although it cannot eliminate all spectral and covariance-related influences \citep{GirardiSchappo2021,Hengen2025}.

\subsection{Band-specific dissociations}
In intracranial recordings, high-gamma and broadband activity occupied distinct criticality-related regimes. High-gamma was more subcritical by branching ratio and more locally complex by LZc, whereas broadband activity sat closer to criticality with stronger long-range temporal correlations \citep{Li2024,Grubov2025}. At the same time, branching ratios in the two bands covaried across subjects, consistent with a systematic band offset constrained by shared subject-level factors.

\subsection{Propofol and sleep occupy distinct fitted-geometry regimes}
In scalp EEG, propofol was associated with tighter spacing and a small spectral-radius shift. Sleep showed a different trajectory: spacing was smallest in REM, whereas relative sensitivity was highest in N3. Thus, in these datasets, pharmacological and physiological routes to reduced arousal occupy distinguishable regions of fitted operator-geometry space; they do not define a single unidimensional ``depth'' axis.

Why REM shows the tightest spacing is not resolved here. One possibility is that REM-specific large-scale dynamics drive eigenvalue crowding; another is that state-dependent spectral/covariance structure interacts with the CSD--PCA--VAR pipeline. The shared-subspace analysis reduces (but does not eliminate) the latter explanation. Discriminating these alternatives will require targeted manipulations, alternative estimators, and larger cohorts \citep{Curic2024,Davila2025}.

\subsection{Interpreting sensitivity-based quantities}
Because autoregressive fitting to autocorrelated signals can inflate sensitivity statistics, we interpret sensitivity primarily in comparative terms (e.g., across conditions or in relation to other fitted-operator summaries). Phase-randomized surrogates support this caution by showing that absolute sensitivity magnitude is not specific to neural temporal structure.

\subsection{Relation to non-normal circuit dynamics}
Non-normal recurrent-network models provide one context in which eigenvalues and eigenvector geometry jointly shape transient responses \citep{Barzon2025,Stroud2024,Mastrogiuseppe2025,Troude2025}. Two observations from the present results are especially relevant. First, Kreiss constants tracked eigenvector condition number ($r=0.91$) but not the gap-overlap composite ($r=0.058$), suggesting that global eigenvector conditioning dominates transient amplification in these fitted operators. Second, propofol increased eigenvector condition number ($d=-0.72$; sedated $>$ awake under the awake-minus-sedated convention), consistent with increased non-normality. These constraints do not identify a circuit mechanism, and because the relevant analyses come from different datasets and aggregation levels, they should not be read as a single causal chain from condition number to state effects.

\subsection{Limitations}
The study has several limitations that constrain interpretation and generalization:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Descriptive scope and identifiability:} all results are correlational summaries of fitted linear operators. The mapping from fitted operator geometry to latent neural mechanisms is not identifiable from these data and estimators alone.
    \item \textbf{Estimator and pipeline dependence:} fitted-operator geometry depends on preprocessing (filtering, referencing/CSD), dimensionality reduction (PCA subspace choice), window length/step, and VAR estimation. Alternative pipelines could shift eigenvalue density and eigenvector conditioning while preserving, attenuating, or reversing qualitative effects.
    \item \textbf{Finite-sample instability of eigenvector geometry:} eigenvector non-orthogonality metrics (e.g., condition numbers) can be numerically unstable near eigenvalue crowding, sensitive to noise, and affected by regularization and scaling choices.
    \item \textbf{Dimension dependence of spacing statistics:} minimum-gap and nearest-neighbor spacing summaries depend on model dimension $p$ and on the estimated subspace; they should be interpreted comparatively within fixed settings rather than as absolute measures of ``proximity'' to a neural singularity.
    \item \textbf{Residual spectral and covariance confounds:} although spacing was largely independent of alpha/delta power in these datasets, residual confounds may remain through broadband spectral shape, cross-channel covariance, and estimator bias in short-window VAR fits.
    \item \textbf{Sample sizes and coverage:} iEEG sample sizes were moderate ($n\approx18$--19) with heterogeneous electrode coverage \citep{Srinivasan2024}; propofol had $n\approx19$--20; sleep had $n=10$. The sleep sample is small in absolute terms, but the headline N3-vs-REM spacing effect ($d=-2.51$) yields $>$99\% power at $\alpha=0.05$ even with $n=10$ paired observations, and the multi-block robustness analysis confirmed that the effect was stable across independent within-subject segments. The primary vulnerability of $n=10$ is therefore generalizability across populations rather than statistical power for the observed effects. Several secondary analyses (spatial effects, PAC, chirality) are underpowered and are treated as exploratory.
    \item \textbf{Replication scope:} the ds004752 SEEG analysis uses a separate dataset (Zurich, SEEG depth electrodes, verbal working memory) from the primary Cogitate iEEG analysis (ECoG surface, visual consciousness). It provides a secondary cross-dataset generalization test across datasets, electrode types, and paradigms. However, both analyses use the same software pipeline, so shared pipeline assumptions remain.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Predictions and future directions}
These results suggest several testable directions. First, if spacing effects are robust, other anesthetics should also reduce spacing under shared-subspace estimation and under alternative spacing metrics \citep{Eisen2026}. Second, seizure transitions provide an opportunity to test whether spacing narrows before ictal onset and widens afterward \citep{Moosavi2025,Grubov2025}. Third, psychedelics provide a complementary perturbation associated with increased complexity and can be tested for increased spacing using pipelines that control for subspace dependence and surrogate baselines \citep{McCulloch2023,Larsen2026,Cardone2024}. More broadly, fitted operator geometry offers a complementary descriptive framework for band-specific and state-dependent variability in criticality-related measures, with conclusions explicitly bounded to the estimators and datasets analyzed here.

\subsubsection{Intracranial EEG (iEEG)}
Band-specific criticality, operator-geometry, chirality, phase--amplitude coupling, channel-sweep, and spatial analyses used intracranial recordings from the COGITATE Experiment~1 adversarial-collaboration dataset \citep{Melloni2023} (Cogitate Consortium; \url{https://cogitate-data.ae.mpg.de/}). Participants were patients with drug-resistant epilepsy who performed a visual consciousness task (face/object/letter/false-font stimuli with task relevance manipulation). Sampling rates ranged from 1024 to 2048~Hz depending on the acquisition system. We included 18--19 subjects depending on the analysis, after excluding subjects that failed quality-control checks. For dynamical-system analyses, we retained up to 30 channels per subject (highest-variance channels after artifact rejection), yielding $30\times30$ Jacobian matrices.

\subsubsection{Propofol scalp EEG}
Awake-versus-sedation analyses used a propofol scalp-EEG dataset from OpenNeuro (ds005620; DOI: \url{https://doi.org/10.18112/openneuro.ds005620.v1.0.0}; CC0) \citep{Maschke2024}. Recordings used a standard 10-05 montage (65 channels) at 5000~Hz. After removing ocular and muscle channels, 62 scalp EEG channels were retained. Each of 20 subjects contributed an eyes-closed awake baseline and up to three sedation runs (rest~1--3). One subject (sub-1037) was excluded based on preprocessing quality criteria, yielding $n=19$--20 depending on the analysis. The primary sedation comparison used run~1 (deepest sedation); pooled results across runs~1--3 were reported as secondary robustness checks.

\subsubsection{Sleep EEG}
Sleep analyses used the ANPHY-Sleep polysomnography dataset from OSF (DOI: \url{https://doi.org/10.17605/OSF.IO/R26FH}) \citep{ANPHYSleepOSF}. The dataset contains full-night recordings from 10 healthy subjects (EPCTL01--EPCTL10) with 93 channels (EEG in the 10-20/10-05 montage plus polygraphy) sampled at 1000~Hz. Sleep was staged in 30-second epochs according to AASM criteria (W, N1, N2, N3, REM). We focused on W, N3, and REM. The primary analysis used the longest contiguous block per state ($\geq$2~min) to avoid concatenation artifacts; a robustness analysis also evaluated the top three contiguous blocks per state per subject (\S\ref{sec:multiblock_sleep}).

\subsection{Signal decomposition and band definitions}

For intracranial recordings, we analyzed two signal definitions: a high-gamma (HG) band (70--150~Hz; 4th-order Butterworth bandpass) and a broadband (BB) signal (1--200~Hz). HG envelope power served as a proxy for local cortical spiking-related activity, whereas BB reflects a broader mixture of synaptic and oscillatory dynamics. Alpha (8--12~Hz) and delta (0.5--4~Hz) power were computed only for control analyses testing whether spacing is independent of conventional oscillatory power.

For scalp EEG (propofol and sleep), signals were bandpass-filtered to 0.5--45~Hz (4th-order zero-phase Butterworth) before spatial transformation and dimensional reduction. This band captures dominant scalp oscillations while excluding much of the muscle and line-noise contamination. VAR estimation therefore operated on broadband CSD-PCA components that reflect mesoscale oscillatory dynamics, which is a different observable from iEEG high-gamma; accordingly, scalp and iEEG results are framed as cross-modal generalization tests rather than direct replications.

\subsection{Preprocessing}

\subsubsection{iEEG preprocessing}
Raw iEEG data were loaded per subject and run (DurR1 for the primary analysis). Bad channels were identified using automated quality-control metrics (variance, kurtosis, and high-frequency noise) and removed. A 60~Hz notch filter was applied to suppress power-line interference. HG and BB envelopes were extracted using the band definitions above. No spatial transformation was applied to iEEG data, as stereotactic electrodes already provide relatively focal recordings. Up to 30 channels per subject were retained for Jacobian estimation, selected by variance ranking.

\subsubsection{Propofol EEG preprocessing}
VEOG, HEOG, and EMG channels were removed, leaving 62 EEG channels. Bad channels were detected using the same automated quality-control pipeline. A 50~Hz notch filter was applied (European recording site). Signals were bandpass-filtered (0.5--45~Hz, 4th-order zero-phase Butterworth), then downsampled from 5000 to 500~Hz using MNE's resampling with built-in anti-aliasing. Current Source Density (CSD) was computed using the surface Laplacian ($\lambda^2=10^{-5}$, stiffness $=4$) to sharpen spatial resolution. PCA was then applied to the CSD-transformed data, retaining 15 components (capturing $>$86\% of variance across subjects). PCA was fitted independently per condition (awake, sedation); all cross-condition comparisons used basis-invariant metrics (eigenvalues, spectral radius, eigenvalue gaps, singular values, effective rank).

\subsubsection{Sleep EEG preprocessing}
Non-EEG channels (SO1, SO2, ZY1, ZY2, ChEMG1, ChEMG2, RLEG$\pm$, LLEG$\pm$, EOG1, EOG2, ECG1, ECG2) were removed. Channels with names that lacked the standard 10-05 montage positions were excluded (typically midline and extended-coverage channels). Bad channels were detected using automated quality control. A 50~Hz notch filter was applied. Signals were bandpass-filtered (0.5--45~Hz) and downsampled from 1000 to 500~Hz. CSD and PCA (15 components) were applied using the same procedure as in the propofol preprocessing. PCA was fitted independently per sleep state.

\subsection{Criticality-related measures}

Four criticality-related measures were computed per subject in the iEEG dataset:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Branching ratio} ($\sigma$): estimated from neuronal avalanche cascades detected on the HG and BB envelopes using a threshold of 3 standard deviations above the mean, with temporal bin width equal to one inter-event interval \citep{Lombardi2023,Srinivasan2024}.
    \item \textbf{Avalanche exponent} ($\tau$): power-law exponent of avalanche size distributions, fitted using the Clauset method with discrete maximum-likelihood estimation and 2500 bootstrap samples \citep{Hengen2025}. Comparison distributions included exponential, lognormal, and truncated power law.
    \item \textbf{Lempel--Ziv complexity} (LZc): computed on binarized HG and BB time series with 100 phase-randomized surrogates for normalization \citep{Li2024,Liang2023,Newman2025}.
    \item \textbf{Detrended fluctuation analysis} (DFA): scaling exponent estimated across logarithmically spaced window sizes \citep{Grubov2025,Angiolelli2025,Mesquita2021}. An exponent of $\alpha=1.5$ indicates long-range correlations; $\alpha=0.5$ indicates uncorrelated noise.
\end{enumerate}

HG and BB measures were compared using paired $t$ tests across subjects and linear mixed-effects models with subject as a random intercept. Decoding reversal was examined as a function of channel subset size ($n=5,\,10,\,20$ channels) using LDA classifiers in a leave-one-block-out cross-validation design.

\subsection{Linear dynamical-system geometry}

For each subject, a first-order vector autoregressive (VAR(1)) model was fitted using sliding windows of 500~ms with 100~ms step size (i.e., 80\% overlap). VAR(1) was chosen for computational tractability and because it provides a common baseline linear approximation that can be estimated robustly in short windows; however, it is a misspecified model for nonlinear, non-stationary neural systems and should be interpreted as a local linear summary rather than a generative description. In each window, the multivariate time series $\mathbf{x}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^{p}$ (where $p=30$ for iEEG, $p=15$ for scalp EEG) was modeled as $\mathbf{x}(t+1) = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}(t) + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(t)$, where $\mathbf{A}$ is the $p\times p$ coefficient matrix estimated by ordinary least squares.

Let $\{\lambda_i\}_{i=1}^p$ denote the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{V}$ the matrix of (right) eigenvectors such that $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{V}=\mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$. From each fitted $\mathbf{A}$, we extracted:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Spectral radius} ($\rho$): $\rho=\max_i |\lambda_i|$.
    \item \textbf{Minimum eigenvalue spacing} (``gap''): $\mathrm{gap}=\min_{i<j} |\lambda_i-\lambda_j|$. Because this statistic is dimension-dependent, it is interpreted comparatively within a fixed model dimension and a preprocessing pipeline.
    \item \textbf{Eigenvector condition number}: $\kappa(\mathbf{V})=\|\mathbf{V}\|_2\,\|\mathbf{V}^{-1}\|_2$, computed using the complex-valued eigendecomposition. Large $\kappa(\mathbf{V})$ indicates strong eigenvector non-orthogonality and numerical sensitivity.
    \item \textbf{Petermann factor (window summary)}: for each eigenmode, left eigenvectors $\mathbf{w}_i$ were computed from $\mathbf{A}^\top$ and paired to right eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_i$ by eigenvalue matching. After bi-orthonormal normalization ($\mathbf{w}_i^\top\mathbf{v}_i=1$), a per-mode Petermann factor was computed as $K_i=\|\mathbf{v}_i\|_2\,\|\mathbf{w}_i\|_2$, and the window-level Petermann factor was summarized as $\max_i K_i$.
    \item \textbf{ND score}: a window-level near-degeneracy composite defined as the average of two $z$-scored terms computed within subject and condition: $\mathrm{ND} = \tfrac{1}{2}\big[z(-\log_{10}(\mathrm{gap}+\epsilon)) + z(\log_{10}(\kappa(\mathbf{V})+\epsilon))\big]$, with $\epsilon=10^{-12}$ for numerical stability. This composite summary of eigenvalue near-degeneracy and eigenvector non-orthogonality is used purely as a descriptive compact summary, not as evidence for a mathematically exact exceptional point.
    \item \textbf{Effective rank}: participation ratio and exponential rank of the Jacobian singular value spectrum, measuring effective dimensionality.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Dimension dependence of spacing summaries.} Eigenvalue-spacing statistics depend on model dimension $p$ and on the estimated subspace (e.g., PCA choices). Throughout, we interpret minimum-gap and nearest-neighbor spacing summaries comparatively (between conditions and across subjects) under fixed pipeline settings, rather than as absolute measurements of latent neural singularities.

\paragraph{Spectral sensitivity.} Spectral sensitivity was defined as the within-subject Pearson correlation between spectral radius and minimum eigenvalue spacing across windows. All within-subject correlations reported autocorrelation-adjusted effective sample sizes ($n_\mathrm{eff}$) using Bartlett's formula: $n_\mathrm{eff} = n(1-\rho_x\rho_y)/(1+\rho_x\rho_y)$, where $\rho_x,\rho_y$ are lag-1 autocorrelations of the two series. $p$-values were recomputed at the adjusted degrees of freedom.

\paragraph{Cross-state comparability and subspace dependence.} For scalp EEG, the primary pipeline fitted PCA independently per condition/state. Because this changes the estimated subspace as well as coordinates, between-state differences in eigenvalue geometry are interpreted as descriptive summaries of best linear dynamics \emph{within each state-specific subspace}. To test whether the headline state effects were artifacts of state-specific coordinate systems, we also performed a robustness analysis of shared-subspace PCA. Within each subject, PCA was fit once on the pooled CSD-transformed data across all states, and each state was then projected onto the common subspace before VAR estimation. Both per-state and shared-subspace results are reported.

State-contrast analyses (task-relevant vs.\ task-irrelevant) used a circular-shift permutation test (5000 permutations) to preserve the temporal autocorrelation structure.

\subsection{Chirality and eigenvalue-trajectory summaries}

For each subject, we tracked the two eigenvalues with the smallest gap across consecutive windows, forming a trajectory in the complex plane. Because eigenvalue ordering can change between windows (label-switching), the closest-gap pair was identified independently per window, and eigenvector continuity across windows was enforced by maximum-overlap matching with phase gauge-fixing; the most frequently occurring gap pair across windows was used as the stable reference pair for trajectory summaries. From this trajectory, we computed:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Chirality index}: the fraction of windows in which the eigenvalue pair exhibited complex splitting (i.e., nonzero imaginary-part difference).
    \item \textbf{Trajectory winding-style summary}: the cumulative angular change of the eigenvalue-pair difference vector across windows \citep{RafiUlIslam2024}. This is reported as a descriptive trajectory statistic and is not interpreted as a formally defined topological invariant of an underlying physical system.
    \item \textbf{Phase-like trajectory summary}: a geometric-phase-style summary accumulated along the trajectory. This quantity is used descriptively and is not taken as evidence for non-Hermitian topological structure.
    \item \textbf{Circular variance}: a measure of directional dispersion in the trajectory.
    \item \textbf{Asymmetry ratio}: the ratio of antisymmetric to total power in the decomposition $\mathbf{A}=(\mathbf{A}+\mathbf{A}^\top)/2+(\mathbf{A}-\mathbf{A}^\top)/2$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-frequency coupling}

Phase--amplitude coupling (PAC) was estimated between low-frequency phase carriers and the high-gamma amplitude envelope using the modulation index \citep{Daume2024,Sakakura2023}. PAC was correlated with criticality measures across subjects to test for selective coupling.

\subsection{Statistics}

Primary inferential statistics included paired $t$ tests with Cohen's $d$ effect sizes, Pearson and Spearman correlations, and linear mixed-effects models (with subject as a random intercept) for band comparisons.

\paragraph{Primary hypotheses and multiplicity.} To reduce analytic flexibility, we designate the following as primary endpoints: (H1) HG vs.\ BB difference in branching ratio $\sigma$; (H2) cross-subject association between $\sigma$ and ND score; (H3) state effect on minimum eigenvalue spacing in propofol (awake vs.\ sedated) and sleep (W vs.\ N3 vs.\ REM); (H4) near-zero within-state correlations between spacing and alpha (propofol) / delta (sleep). Other metrics (PAC, chirality-related observables, decoding reversal, and sensitivity) are treated as secondary/exploratory.

False-discovery-rate (FDR) correction \citep{Benjamini1995} was applied to grouped families of tests within each analysis (e.g., all sleep pairwise state contrasts; all secondary correlations tested jointly). The significance threshold was $\alpha=0.05$ throughout, applied to FDR-corrected $p$-values where multiple tests were performed.

For the SVD dimension analysis, a random-matrix null model was used to control for mathematical coupling between ND score and effective rank: random matrices matching the empirical spectral radius distribution were generated, and their rank--ND-score correlation served as the null distribution.

For the Petermann noise analysis, cross-correlation was computed using NaN-aware methods with a non-overlapping stride (stride $=5$ windows) for the Granger-like precedence test, avoiding temporal leakage from overlapping windows.

\subsection{Robustness analyses}

Robustness checks were performed to evaluate identifiability, pipeline dependence, and cross-state comparability:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Surrogate controls (spectral sensitivity identifiability):} For 5 propofol subjects (subset: 1010, 1017, 1033, 1055, 1068), we generated 200 phase-randomized surrogates per subject. Phase randomization preserved the power spectrum and cross-channel coherence but destroyed fine temporal structure. VAR(1) fitting and spectral sensitivity computation were repeated on each surrogate; increasing surrogate count did not change the interpretive conclusion reported in Results.
    \item \textbf{Partial regression with alpha control:} The propofol change--change correlation (change in relative sensitivity vs.\ change in spacing) was recomputed as a partial correlation controlling for change in alpha power.
    \item \textbf{PCA dimensionality robustness:} The propofol pipeline was re-run with 10 and 20 PCA components (in addition to the standard 15).
    \item \textbf{Shared-subspace PCA:} Within each subject, PCA was fit once on pooled CSD-transformed data across all states, and each state was projected into the common subspace before VAR estimation.
    \item \textbf{Alternative local-spacing metrics:} Nearest-neighbor spacing was summarized by the median and the 10th percentile in addition to the minimum gap.
    \item \textbf{Remaining robustness extensions (recommended):} window-length/overlap sweeps; alternative linear estimators (ridge-regularized VAR, state-space/subspace identification) to assess stability of $\rho$, spacing, and ND score.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, interpretation is anchored in (i) robustness to shared-subspace estimation (mitigating state-dependent PCA coordinate changes) and (ii) concordance across spacing summaries (minimum gap, median nearest-neighbor spacing, and low-quantile nearest-neighbor spacing). In contrast, the surrogate analysis indicates that absolute spectral sensitivity can arise from autoregressive fitting to autocorrelated signals, motivating comparative rather than standalone interpretation of sensitivity magnitude.

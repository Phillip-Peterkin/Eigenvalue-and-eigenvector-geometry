Criticality-related measures are widely used to summarize near-critical brain dynamics. We asked how these measures relate to eigenvalue- and eigenvector-geometry summaries of sliding-window VAR(1) operators fitted under a fixed preprocessing and dimensionality-reduction pipeline. In intracranial recordings, high-gamma activity was more subcritical than broadband activity (branching ratio $\sigma_{\mathrm{HG}}=0.974$ vs.~$\sigma_{\mathrm{BB}}=0.991$; mixed-effects $p<10^{-8}$). Across subjects, a composite operator-geometry score (``EP score,'' combining eigenvalue proximity and eigenvector non-orthogonality) covaried strongly with branching ratio ($r=0.86$, $p<10^{-5}$) and inversely with Lempel--Ziv complexity ($r=-0.68$, $p=0.002$). Phase-randomized surrogates constrained interpretation of sensitivity: absolute spectral sensitivity was not specific to neural temporal structure, so sensitivity results are interpreted comparatively. Together, fitted operator-geometry summaries provide a complementary set of descriptive coordinates for variation across subjects, frequency bands, and states in these datasets.

\section*{Significance}
Criticality-related measures summarize aspects of near-critical brain dynamics but do not directly describe the geometry of fitted multivariate operators. Using human iEEG and scalp EEG, we show that eigenvalue-spacing and eigenvector-geometry summaries derived from sliding-window VAR(1) models covary with conventional criticality statistics across individuals and separate propofol sedation and sleep stages under this analysis pipeline in ways that are not explained by alpha- or delta-power. The main state effects persist under shared-subspace estimation, and an independent SEEG replication in ds004752 (a separate Zurich dataset) supports stable geometry--dynamics relationships more strongly than task-condition contrasts. These fitted operator-geometry summaries provide additional descriptive coordinates for inter-individual and state-dependent variability in these datasets.

\section{Introduction}

Neural systems are widely hypothesized to operate near a critical regime that balances stability with responsiveness. Evidence spans neuronal avalanche statistics, long-range temporal correlations, and signal-complexity measures across species, recording modalities, and behavioral states \citep{Beggs2003,Shriki2013,Lombardi2023,Srinivasan2024,Xin2025,Hengen2025,GirardiSchappo2021,Hardstone2012,Grubov2025,Mesquita2021,LempelZiv1976,Schartner2015,Li2024,Newman2025,Angiolelli2025}. Departures from near-critical dynamics accompany altered arousal, pharmacological sedation, and neuropsychiatric conditions \citep{Gervais2023,Maschke2024,Angiolelli2025}.

Two converging lines of work motivate the present study. First, criticality-related measures track state variation across pharmacological and physiological perturbations. Propofol and xenon shift multiple criticality-related summaries away from a near-critical regime, whereas ketamine can preserve near-critical statistics in some analyses \citep{Maschke2024,Curic2024}. During sleep, avalanche scaling can remain approximately intact across stages even as avalanche density and temporal patterning vary \citep{Pereira2023,Huo2024}. These observations motivate quantifying ``distance from criticality'' as a descriptive coordinate of state, while leaving open what that distance corresponds to geometrically and how it depends on estimators and preprocessing.

Second, linear dynamical analyses offer a complementary descriptive perspective on state variation. Propofol shifts the eigenvalue spectrum of fitted operators toward instability \citep{Eisen2024,Eisen2026}, and chemically distinct anesthetics can produce distinguishable spectral signatures \citep{Davila2025,BerjagaBuisan2025}. Yet eigenvalue location alone incompletely describes non-normal systems, where eigenvector geometry---alignment, condition number, and transient amplification capacity---can shape observed dynamics as much as eigenvalue placement \citep{Barzon2025,Stroud2024,Troude2025,Horrocks2024,Galgali2023,Jarne2023}.

A practical limitation of many state and criticality analyses is that they summarize time series with scalar measures (e.g., avalanche statistics or band-limited power) without directly characterizing the geometry of fitted multivariate dynamics. Here we treat sliding-window VAR(1) operators as local linear summaries and extract operator-geometry statistics---minimum eigenvalue spacing and eigenvector non-orthogonality (via an eigenvector condition number)---as descriptive coordinates. These quantities are properties of fitted linear operators under a specific preprocessing and dimensionality-reduction pipeline; they are estimator-dependent and are interpreted as correlational summaries rather than as evidence for neural singularities, formal non-Hermitian topology, or mechanistic ``exceptional points''.

We address three questions in human electrophysiological data. (1) Do high-gamma and broadband signals occupy distinct criticality-related regimes within intracranial recordings? (2) Across subjects, do criticality measures covary with fitted operator-geometry summaries? (3) Across state transitions (propofol anesthesia and natural sleep), do fitted geometry summaries change in ways that are not reducible to conventional alpha/delta power under the same pipeline?

We report five main findings. High-gamma is more subcritical than broadband, with dissociations among branching ratio, complexity, and long-range correlations. Branching ratio covaries strongly with a composite operator-geometry score across subjects. Minimum eigenvalue spacing is largely independent of alpha and delta power. Propofol and sleep occupy distinguishable regions of fitted operator-geometry summaries in these datasets. These state-related effects were robust to shared-subspace estimation and to spacing summaries beyond the minimum gap.

\section{Results}

\subsection{High-gamma and broadband activity occupy different criticality-related regimes}
We first asked whether criticality-related measures differ between high-gamma (HG) and broadband (BB) activity in intracranial recordings. Across subjects, HG was more subcritical than BB by branching ratio. Mean branching ratio was $\sigma_{\mathrm{HG}}=0.9735$ and $\sigma_{\mathrm{BB}}=0.9908$ (paired $t=-5.74$, $p=8.9\times10^{-6}$); a mixed-effects model confirmed a robust band effect (coefficient $=-0.01727$, $p=9.3\times10^{-9}$).

Other measures dissociated across bands. Lempel--Ziv complexity was higher in HG than BB (mean difference $=0.0572$, $t=5.24$, $p=2.9\times10^{-5}$; mixed-effects $p=1.6\times10^{-7}$). In contrast, detrended fluctuation analysis (DFA) was higher in BB than HG (mean difference $=-0.2568$, $t=-10.04$, $p=1.1\times10^{-9}$; mixed-effects $p=1.0\times10^{-23}$). The avalanche exponent $\tau$ was lower in HG than BB, but the group-level difference was marginal (paired $p=0.065$; mixed-effects $p=0.052$). Within the same subjects and sessions, these dissociations indicate that HG is more subcritical and more locally complex, whereas BB sits closer to criticality with stronger long-range temporal structure \citep{Grubov2025}.

Despite these dissociations, HG and BB criticality measures were not independent across subjects: a mixed-effects model showed that HG branching ratio significantly predicted BB branching ratio (coefficient $=0.212$, $p=5.4\times 10^{-5}$), indicating that the two bands are coupled but systematically offset rather than fully decoupled.

A secondary analysis of decoding reversal---the hypothesis that band-specific criticality would predict classification accuracy---yielded limited support. Reversal counts were numerically higher in HG than BB, but paired comparisons were not significant ($p>0.48$ across channel-set sizes), with a weak signal confined to the smallest subset ($n=5$ channels; $r=-0.456$, $p=0.050$). We treat this as exploratory.

\subsection{Fitted operator geometry aligns with criticality and complexity}
We next asked whether criticality-related measures covary with summaries of fitted operator geometry. Across 18 subjects, branching ratio was strongly associated with a composite operator-geometry score (``EP score,'' combining eigenvalue proximity and eigenvector non-orthogonality; $r=0.860$, $p=4.8\times10^{-6}$). This association was robust to leave-one-subject-out jackknife resampling: all 18 of 18 recomputed correlations remained significant, and the recomputed correlation values ranged from $r=0.792$ to $r=0.889$. Branching ratio also correlated negatively with minimum eigenvalue spacing (gap; $r=-0.588$, $p=0.0102$).

Complexity showed the opposite pattern. LZc correlated negatively with EP score ($r=-0.684$, $p=0.00174$) and positively with spacing ($r=0.745$, $p=3.9\times10^{-4}$). The avalanche exponent $\tau$ also correlated with EP score ($r=0.526$, $p=0.025$), although less strongly than $\sigma$. These correlations place criticality-related summaries and fitted operator-geometry summaries in a shared descriptive space but do not identify a mechanistic cause, a unique operator, or a mathematically exact exceptional point \citep{Bergholtz2021}.

Secondary geometric measures showed a similar direction of association. The chirality index correlated positively with $\sigma$ ($r=0.526$, $p=0.025$) and negatively with LZc ($r=-0.570$, $p=0.013$), indicating that eigenvalue-trajectory summaries covary with criticality-related measures \citep{RafiUlIslam2024,Ding2024}. By contrast, a secondary spectral-radius--overlap test was not significant ($p=0.542$); this specific test statistic was not archived in the JSON artifacts, so it should be treated as a non-central supporting analysis.

\subsection{Minimum eigenvalue spacing is largely independent of alpha and delta power}
A key interpretive question is whether minimum eigenvalue spacing tracks conventional oscillatory power. We tested this using alpha power in the propofol dataset and delta power in the sleep dataset.

In propofol EEG, spacing--alpha correlations were near zero in both states (mean $r=-0.013$ when sedated; mean $r=0.027$ when awake). The maximum absolute correlation across subjects was 0.103, and no subject exceeded $|r|>0.3$. Although a paired comparison detected a shift in this near-zero correlation (paired $t=-4.85$, $p=1.1\times10^{-4}$, Cohen's $d=-1.09$), the within-state results indicate that alpha power does not account for spacing.

In sleep EEG, spacing--delta correlations were similarly negligible (mean $r=-0.001\pm0.013$), and no subject showed $|r|>0.3$. In these datasets, minimum eigenvalue spacing varies with state while remaining largely orthogonal to alpha and delta power, reducing (but not eliminating) the possibility that spacing is a proxy for band-limited power. This qualitative independence was also observed under shared-subspace estimation and when spacing was summarized by median and low-quantile nearest-neighbor metrics, indicating that it is not restricted to the single minimum-gap statistic.

\subsection{Propofol is associated with tighter spacing and a small spectral-radius shift}
We next tested whether propofol anesthesia is associated with changes in fitted operator geometry in scalp EEG. Relative to wakefulness, propofol increased the mean spectral radius from 0.9980 to 1.0025 (mean difference $=-0.0045$, $t=-7.44$, $p=4.8\times10^{-7}$, $d=-1.66$). Because spectral-radius values near or above unity can reflect estimation bias as well as genuine dynamical changes, this effect is interpreted alongside spacing and rank measures.

Propofol also reduced minimum eigenvalue spacing from approximately 0.0122 to 0.0114 (paired $t=3.16$, $p=0.0052$, $d=0.71$). A similar pooled effect was observed across sedation runs ($p=0.0081$, $d=0.66$). Effective rank changed little and was not significant ($p=0.185$). In this pipeline, propofol is associated with tighter eigenvalue spacing without strong evidence for a rank collapse, extending prior eigenvalue-spectrum results under anesthesia \citep{Eisen2024,Eisen2026} by highlighting spacing as an additional descriptive summary of state-related operator organization. The headline spacing effect was also present under shared-subspace PCA and when spacing was summarized by median and low-quantile nearest-neighbor metrics (\S2.6).

Because Kreiss constants can increase when operators move closer to instability, we next asked whether the propofol-related amplification shift remained after controlling for stability margin. Within each subject, we residualized per-window log Kreiss constant against the log spectral-radius gap pooled across conditions. The wake-to-sedation increase in residual Kreiss remained large (Cohen's $d=1.82$, Wilcoxon $p<10^{-5}$, 19 of 20 subjects positive), indicating that the propofol-associated amplification shift is not explained solely by movement toward instability. Across the subset with all three sedation runs, Kreiss metrics were also consistently elevated relative to wakefulness in each run (all pairwise $d>0.88$, all $p<0.006$), although strict monotonic ordering across runs was uncommon (2 of 14 subjects).

\subsection{Sleep shows a distinct pattern from propofol}
We then analyzed natural sleep to ask whether physiological state transitions mirror the propofol pattern.

Minimum eigenvalue spacing was smallest in REM sleep. Awake versus N3 showed no reliable difference (0.0132 vs.\ 0.0127, $p=0.216$, $d=-0.42$). In contrast, N3 versus REM showed a narrowing (0.0127 vs.\ 0.0111, $p=2.4\times10^{-5}$, false-discovery-rate significant, $d=-2.51$), and awake versus REM was also significant ($p=8.6\times10^{-5}$, $d=-2.13$). Thus, REM rather than N3 occupied the tightest-spacing regime.

Relative spectral sensitivity showed a different ordering. Awake sensitivity was low and not significant (mean $r=0.021\pm0.088$, $t=0.72$, $p=0.492$). N3 sensitivity was highest (mean $r=0.098\pm0.011$, $t=27.49$, $p<10^{-6}$), and REM sensitivity was intermediate but still significant (mean $r=0.074\pm0.019$, $t=11.44$, $p=10^{-6}$). The N3-versus-REM difference was significant ($t=3.57$, $p=0.006$, $d=1.13$), whereas awake versus REM was not ($p=0.086$). In this dataset, REM shows the tightest spacing while N3 shows the highest relative sensitivity; this dissociation indicates that pharmacological and physiological routes to reduced arousal engage the fitted operator geometry differently \citep{Pereira2023,Huo2024}. The key REM-related spacing contrasts also survived shared-subspace PCA and were supported by low-quantile nearest-neighbor spacing metrics (\S2.6).

\subsection{Multi-block sleep robustness analysis}\label{sec:multiblock_sleep}
To assess sensitivity to block selection in sleep staging, we repeated the sleep state-contrast analyses using the top three contiguous blocks per state per subject (rather than only the single longest block) and aggregated within subject before group testing. The principal REM-related spacing effects (N3 vs.~REM and Awake vs.~REM) were consistent with the primary analysis, indicating that the sleep geometry effects are not driven by a single selected block. This multi-block analysis also yielded an Awake vs.~N3 spacing difference; because this result depends on block aggregation choices and within-subject dependence across blocks, we report it as a robustness outcome rather than as a new headline finding.

\subsection{Secondary analyses: cross-frequency coupling, chirality, and spatial effects}
Several additional analyses were informative but not central to the primary hypotheses.

Phase--amplitude coupling (PAC) did not correlate with branching ratio ($r=-0.059$, $p=0.816$) and did not differ between task-relevant and irrelevant epochs ($p=0.316$). PAC did correlate with the avalanche exponent $\tau$ ($r=0.526$, $p=0.025$), consistent with a selective relationship to temporal scaling rather than to all criticality-related measures \citep{Daume2024,Sakakura2023}.

Chirality-related summaries showed associations in the same direction as the operator-geometry results. The chirality index correlated positively with $\sigma$ and negatively with LZc. Per-subject chirality values are archived; the reported group mean requires recomputing the aggregate summary from those per-subject results. These associations are descriptive and do not establish chirality as an independent mechanism.

Spatially, the sign reversal in the task-related LZc contrast was present in four of five deeply sampled subjects, and greater posterior coverage tended to strengthen the effect ($r=0.338$, $p=0.158$). Given the small sample and heterogeneous coverage, we treat these observations as suggestive. Secondary measures show qualitatively consistent patterns but are not used as primary evidence for the main claims.

\subsection{Robustness analyses constrain interpretation}
We performed robustness checks that directly affect how the operator-geometry results should be interpreted.


A shared-subspace robustness analysis substantially reduced the concern that the reported geometry effects were driven by state-specific principal component analysis bases. Instead of fitting PCA independently per condition, we pooled all states' CSD-transformed data within each subject, fit PCA once on the pooled matrix, and projected each state into the common subspace before VAR estimation. In propofol data, the spectral-radius shift survived with a slightly larger effect size ($d=-1.72$ vs.\ $d=-1.66$ under per-state PCA), and minimum eigenvalue spacing showed a similar strengthening ($d=0.78$ vs.\ $d=0.71$). In sleep data, the key N3-versus-REM spacing difference was attenuated only modestly ($d=-2.39$ vs.\ $d=-2.51$), and the awake-versus-REM contrast likewise survived ($d=-2.03$ vs.\ $d=-2.13$). All shared-subspace comparisons that were significant under per-state PCA remained false-discovery-rate significant ($q<0.05$).

Robustness to median and low-quantile nearest-neighbor spacing indicates that the reported state effects reflect a broader pattern of eigenvalue crowding rather than dependence on a single minimum-gap statistic. In the shared-subspace analysis, propofol-versus-awake differences were significant for median nearest-neighbor spacing ($d=0.78$) and 10th-percentile nearest-neighbor spacing ($d=0.86$), both FDR-corrected. In sleep, the 10th-percentile metric confirmed the N3-versus-REM contrast ($d=-2.30$, FDR significant), while the median metric showed significant awake-versus-N3 ($d=-2.69$) and awake-versus-REM ($d=-2.65$) separations. A separate multi-block sleep robustness analysis (top three contiguous blocks per state) supported the headline REM-related spacing effects and suggested that the awake-versus-N3 contrast can reach significance under block-aggregated estimation (\S\ref{sec:multiblock_sleep}); we treat this as an additional robustness outcome rather than a revised headline effect.

\subsection{Independent SEEG replication (ds004752)}

\section{Discussion}

This study shows that fitted operator geometry can serve as an additional descriptive coordinate system for state- and subject-level variation in human electrophysiology. Across the analyzed datasets, sliding-window VAR(1) fits yield operator-geometry summaries that (i) differ between high-gamma and broadband signals within subjects, (ii) covary with criticality-related measures across subjects, and (iii) separate propofol sedation from wakefulness and distinguish sleep stages in these data. All such separations are statements about fitted linear operators produced by the specified preprocessing, dimensionality-reduction, and estimation pipeline, and they should be interpreted as correlational summaries rather than mechanistic inferences.

\subsection{Fitted operator geometry aligns with criticality and complexity}
The strongest association is between branching ratio and a composite operator-geometry score (EP score; $r=0.86$). Subjects whose avalanche statistics were closer to a near-critical regime also tended to show tighter eigenvalue spacing and greater eigenvector non-orthogonality in fitted operators. This covariation is consistent with prior work showing that eigenvector geometry can influence transient amplification and numerical sensitivity in non-normal systems \citep{Schomerus2024,Kullig2025,Fyodorov2025,Zou2024}, but here it should be interpreted as an estimator-dependent association rather than a mechanistic inference.

\paragraph{Why ``EP score'' is only shorthand.} EP score is a compact composite of two fitted-operator summaries: eigenvalue crowding (near-degeneracy-style proximity) and eigenvector non-orthogonality. The term is used as shorthand for this combination and to connect with prior non-Hermitian linear-systems literature. However, the present analyses do not establish a mathematically exact exceptional point, a topological winding number, or sensing-like phenomena in neural dynamics. Instead, EP score should be read as a descriptive operator-geometry coordinate within the specified pipeline.

Minimum eigenvalue spacing was also largely independent of alpha (propofol) and delta (sleep) power in these datasets. This reduces (but does not eliminate) a common confound in state comparisons, where apparent ``dynamical'' effects can reflect changes in oscillatory power or covariance structure rather than changes in fitted operator organization \citep{GirardiSchappo2021,Hengen2025}.

\subsection{Band-specific dissociations}
Within intracranial recordings, high-gamma and broadband signals showed distinct criticality-related profiles. HG was more subcritical by branching ratio and more locally complex by LZc; BB was closer to a near-critical regime with stronger long-range temporal correlations \citep{Li2024,Grubov2025}. At the same time, HG and BB branching ratios covaried across subjects, suggesting band-specific regimes that remain constrained by shared subject-level factors.

\subsection{Propofol and sleep occupy distinct fitted-geometry regimes}
In scalp EEG, propofol was associated with reduced minimum eigenvalue spacing and a small spectral-radius shift. Sleep showed a different ordering: spacing was smallest in REM, while relative sensitivity was highest in N3. In these data, the fitted operator-geometry summaries occupy distinguishable regions across states; they do not establish a unidimensional ordering of arousal depth or a general theory of consciousness.

Why REM rather than N3 exhibits the tightest spacing is not resolved by the present data. Plausible explanations include REM-associated changes in large-scale dynamics and the possibility that state-specific spectral/covariance structure interacts with the CSD--PCA--VAR pipeline to affect eigenvalue crowding. The shared-subspace analysis (\S2.6) reduces, but does not eliminate, the latter possibility. Distinguishing among these alternatives will require targeted experimental manipulations, alternative estimators, and larger cohorts \citep{Curic2024,Davila2025}.

\subsection{Interpreting sensitivity-based quantities}
Sensitivity magnitude can be inflated by autoregressive fitting to autocorrelated signals. Consistent with this, phase-randomized surrogates suggested that the absolute magnitude of the spectral-sensitivity statistic is not specific to neural temporal structure. Accordingly, sensitivity-based results are interpreted comparatively (e.g., between conditions or in relation to other fitted-operator summaries) rather than as standalone markers.

\subsection{Relation to non-normal circuit dynamics}
At the circuit level, non-normal dynamics in recurrent networks provide one modeling context in which eigenvalues and eigenvector geometry jointly shape transient responses \citep{Barzon2025,Stroud2024,Mastrogiuseppe2025,Troude2025}. The present results do not identify a circuit mechanism; instead, they motivate model-based work that asks which estimator choices and which classes of network models reproduce the observed covariation patterns.

\subsection{Limitations}
The study has several limitations that constrain interpretation and generalization:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Descriptive scope and identifiability:} all results are correlational summaries of fitted linear operators. The mapping from fitted operator geometry to latent neural mechanisms is not identifiable from these data and estimators alone.
    \item \textbf{Estimator and pipeline dependence:} fitted-operator geometry depends on preprocessing (filtering, referencing/CSD), dimensionality reduction (PCA subspace choice), window length/step, and VAR estimation. Alternative pipelines could shift eigenvalue density and eigenvector conditioning while preserving, attenuating, or reversing qualitative effects.
    \item \textbf{Finite-sample instability of eigenvector geometry:} eigenvector non-orthogonality metrics (e.g., condition numbers) can be numerically unstable near eigenvalue crowding, sensitive to noise, and affected by regularization and scaling choices.
    \item \textbf{Dimension dependence of spacing statistics:} minimum-gap and nearest-neighbor spacing summaries depend on model dimension $p$ and on the estimated subspace; they should be interpreted comparatively within fixed settings rather than as absolute measures of ``proximity'' to a neural singularity.
    \item \textbf{Residual spectral and covariance confounds:} although spacing was largely independent of alpha/delta power in these datasets, residual confounds may remain through broadband spectral shape, cross-channel covariance, and estimator bias in short-window VAR fits.
    \item \textbf{Sample sizes and coverage:} iEEG sample sizes were moderate ($n\approx18$--19) with heterogeneous electrode coverage \citep{Srinivasan2024}; propofol had $n\approx19$--20; sleep had $n=10$. Several secondary analyses (decoding reversal, spatial effects, PAC, chirality) are underpowered and are treated as exploratory.
    \item \textbf{Replication scope:} the ds004752 SEEG analysis uses a completely separate dataset (Zurich, SEEG depth electrodes, verbal working memory) from the primary Cogitate iEEG analysis (ECoG surface, visual consciousness). It constitutes an independent replication across datasets, labs, electrode types, and paradigms. However, both analyses use the same software pipeline, so shared pipeline assumptions remain.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Predictions and future directions}
The results motivate several testable directions. First, if spacing effects are robust, other anesthetics should show reduced spacing under shared-subspace and alternative-metric pipelines \citep{Eisen2026}. Second, seizure transitions provide a context to test whether spacing narrows before ictal onset and widens afterward \citep{Moosavi2025,Grubov2025}. Third, psychedelics provide a complementary perturbation associated with increased complexity and can be tested for increased spacing using pipelines that control for subspace dependence and surrogate baselines \citep{McCulloch2023,Larsen2026,Cardone2024}. Taken together, these results motivate fitted operator geometry as a complementary descriptive framework for band-specific and state-dependent variability in criticality-related measures, with conclusions explicitly bounded to the estimators and datasets analyzed here.

\subsubsection{Intracranial EEG (iEEG)}
Band-specific criticality, EP-inspired operator-geometry summaries, chirality, phase--amplitude coupling, channel-sweep, and spatial analyses used stereotactic EEG data from the COGITATE Experiment~1 adversarial-collaboration dataset \citep{Melloni2023}, available from the Cogitate Consortium (\url{https://cogitate-data.ae.mpg.de/}). The dataset contains ECoG recordings from patients with drug-resistant epilepsy who performed a visual consciousness task (face/object/letter/false-font stimuli with task relevance manipulation). Native sampling rates ranged from 1024 to 2048~Hz depending on the acquisition system. A total of 18--19 subjects were included depending on the specific analysis, after excluding subjects that failed quality-control checks for channel count or signal integrity. For the dynamical-system analyses, up to 30 channels were retained per subject (the 30 channels with highest variance after artifact rejection), yielding Jacobian matrices of dimension $30\times30$.

\subsubsection{Propofol scalp EEG}
Awake-versus-sedation comparisons used a propofol sedation scalp-EEG dataset available on OpenNeuro as ds005620 (DOI: \url{https://doi.org/10.18112/openneuro.ds005620.v1.0.0}; CC0 license) \citep{Maschke2024}. The dataset contains 65 recorded channels (standard 10-05 montage), of which 62 scalp EEG channels were retained for analysis after removal of ocular and muscle channels, from 20 subjects recorded at 5000~Hz using a BrainVision system. Each subject contributed an eyes-closed awake baseline and up to three propofol sedation runs (rest~1--3). One subject (sub-1037) was excluded based on preprocessing quality criteria, yielding $N=19$--20 depending on the analysis. The primary sedation comparison used run-1 only (deepest sedation level); pooled averages across runs~1--3 were reported as secondary for robustness.

\subsubsection{Sleep EEG}
Sleep analyses used the ANPHY-Sleep polysomnography dataset, downloaded from OSF (DOI: \url{https://doi.org/10.17605/OSF.IO/R26FH}; public access) \citep{ANPHYSleepOSF}. The dataset contains full-night recordings from 10 healthy subjects (EPCTL01--EPCTL28), with 93 channels (EEG in the 10-20/10-05 montage plus polygraphy channels) sampled at 1000~Hz. Sleep staging was performed in 30-second epochs according to AASM criteria, yielding labels for Wake (W), N1, N2, N3, and REM (R). For the present analyses, we used the three states with the clearest theoretical predictions: W, N3, and REM. The primary analysis used the longest contiguous run of consecutive same-stage epochs ($\geq$2~min minimum duration) to avoid concatenation artifacts from non-contiguous epochs; a robustness analysis additionally evaluated the top three contiguous blocks per state per subject (\S\ref{sec:multiblock_sleep}).

\subsection{Signal decomposition and band definitions}

For intracranial recordings, signals were analyzed in two conditions: a high-gamma (HG) band (70--150~Hz, 4th-order Butterworth bandpass) and a broadband (BB) condition (1--200~Hz). HG envelope power served as a proxy for local cortical spiking activity, whereas BB reflected the full range of synaptic and oscillatory dynamics. Alpha-band (8--12~Hz) and delta-band (0.5--4~Hz) power were computed only for control analyses testing eigenvalue-gap independence from conventional oscillatory power.

For scalp EEG (propofol and sleep), signals were bandpass-filtered to 0.5--45~Hz using a 4th-order zero-phase Butterworth filter before spatial and dimensional reduction. This range captures the dominant oscillatory dynamics visible at the scalp while excluding muscle artifacts and line noise harmonics. The Jacobian estimation therefore operated on broadband CSD-PCA components reflecting synaptic currents and mesoscale oscillatory dynamics---a fundamentally different observable from iEEG high-gamma. Results are accordingly framed as cross-modal generalization tests, not direct replications.

\subsection{Preprocessing}

\subsubsection{iEEG preprocessing}
Raw iEEG data were loaded per subject and run (DurR1 for the primary analysis). Bad channels were identified using automated quality-control metrics (variance, kurtosis, and high-frequency noise) and removed. A 60~Hz notch filter was applied to suppress power-line interference. HG and BB envelopes were extracted using the band definitions above. No spatial transformation was applied to iEEG data, as stereotactic electrodes already provide relatively focal recordings. Up to 30 channels per subject were retained for Jacobian estimation, selected by variance ranking.

\subsubsection{Propofol EEG preprocessing}
VEOG, HEOG, and EMG channels were removed, leaving 62 EEG channels. Bad channels were detected using the same automated quality-control pipeline. A 50~Hz notch filter was applied (European recording site). Signals were bandpass-filtered (0.5--45~Hz, 4th-order zero-phase Butterworth), then downsampled from 5000 to 500~Hz using MNE's resampling with built-in anti-aliasing. Current Source Density (CSD) was computed using the surface Laplacian ($\lambda^2=10^{-5}$, stiffness $=4$) to sharpen spatial resolution. PCA was then applied to the CSD-transformed data, retaining 15 components (capturing $>$86\% of variance across subjects). PCA was fitted independently per condition (awake, sedation); all cross-condition comparisons used basis-invariant metrics (eigenvalues, spectral radius, eigenvalue gaps, singular values, effective rank).

\subsubsection{Sleep EEG preprocessing}
Non-EEG channels (SO1, SO2, ZY1, ZY2, ChEMG1, ChEMG2, RLEG$\pm$, LLEG$\pm$, EOG1, EOG2, ECG1, ECG2) were removed. Channels with names lacking standard 10-05 montage positions were dropped (typically midline and extended-coverage channels). Bad channels were detected using automated quality control. A 50~Hz notch filter was applied. Signals were bandpass-filtered (0.5--45~Hz) and downsampled from 1000 to 500~Hz. CSD and PCA (15 components) were applied identically to the propofol pipeline. PCA was fitted independently per sleep state.

\subsection{Criticality-related measures}

Four criticality-related measures were computed per subject in the iEEG dataset:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Branching ratio} ($\sigma$): estimated from neuronal avalanche cascades detected on the HG and BB envelopes using a threshold of 3 standard deviations above the mean, with temporal bin width equal to one inter-event interval \citep{Lombardi2023,Srinivasan2024}.
    \item \textbf{Avalanche exponent} ($\tau$): power-law exponent of avalanche size distributions, fitted using the Clauset method with discrete maximum-likelihood estimation and 2500 bootstrap samples \citep{Hengen2025}. Comparison distributions included exponential, lognormal, and truncated power law.
    \item \textbf{Lempel--Ziv complexity} (LZc): computed on binarized HG and BB time series with 100 phase-randomized surrogates for normalization \citep{Li2024,Liang2023,Newman2025}.
    \item \textbf{Detrended fluctuation analysis} (DFA): scaling exponent estimated across logarithmically spaced window sizes \citep{Grubov2025,Angiolelli2025,Mesquita2021}. An exponent of $\alpha=1.5$ indicates long-range correlations; $\alpha=0.5$ indicates uncorrelated noise.
\end{enumerate}

HG and BB measures were compared using paired $t$ tests across subjects and linear mixed-effects models with subject as a random intercept. Decoding reversal was examined as a function of channel subset size ($n=5,\,10,\,20$ channels) using LDA classifiers in a leave-one-block-out cross-validation design.

\subsection{Linear dynamical-system geometry}

For each subject, a first-order vector autoregressive model (VAR(1)) was fitted in sliding windows of 500~ms with 100~ms steps (i.e., 80\% overlap). VAR(1) was chosen for computational tractability and because it provides a common baseline linear approximation that can be estimated robustly in short windows; however, it is a misspecified model for nonlinear, non-stationary neural systems and should be interpreted as a local linear summary rather than a generative description. In each window, the multivariate time series $\mathbf{x}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^{p}$ (where $p=30$ for iEEG, $p=15$ for scalp EEG) was modeled as $\mathbf{x}(t+1) = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}(t) + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(t)$, where $\mathbf{A}$ is the $p\times p$ coefficient matrix estimated by ordinary least squares.

Let $\{\lambda_i\}_{i=1}^p$ denote the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{V}$ the matrix of (right) eigenvectors such that $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{V}=\mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$. From each fitted $\mathbf{A}$, we extracted:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Spectral radius} ($\rho$): $\rho=\max_i |\lambda_i|$.
    \item \textbf{Minimum eigenvalue spacing} (``gap''): $\mathrm{gap}=\min_{i<j} |\lambda_i-\lambda_j|$. Because this statistic is dimension-dependent, it is interpreted comparatively within a fixed model dimension and preprocessing pipeline.
    \item \textbf{Eigenvector condition number}: $\kappa(\mathbf{V})=\|\mathbf{V}\|_2\,\|\mathbf{V}^{-1}\|_2$, computed using the complex-valued eigendecomposition. Large $\kappa(\mathbf{V})$ indicates strong eigenvector non-orthogonality and numerical sensitivity.
    \item \textbf{Petermann factor (window summary)}: for each eigenmode, left eigenvectors $\mathbf{w}_i$ were computed from $\mathbf{A}^\top$ and paired to right eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_i$ by eigenvalue matching. After bi-orthonormal normalization ($\mathbf{w}_i^\top\mathbf{v}_i=1$), a per-mode Petermann factor was computed as $K_i=\|\mathbf{v}_i\|_2\,\|\mathbf{w}_i\|_2$, and the window-level Petermann factor was summarized as $\max_i K_i$.
    \item \textbf{EP score}: a window-level composite defined as the average of two $z$-scored terms computed within subject and condition: $\mathrm{EP} = \tfrac{1}{2}\big[z(-\log_{10}(\mathrm{gap}+\epsilon)) + z(\log_{10}(\kappa(\mathbf{V})+\epsilon))\big]$, with $\epsilon=10^{-12}$ for numerical stability. This EP-inspired geometry score (a composite summary of eigenvalue proximity and eigenvector non-orthogonality) is used purely as a descriptive compact summary, not as evidence for a mathematically exact exceptional point.
    \item \textbf{Effective rank}: participation ratio and exponential rank of the Jacobian singular value spectrum, measuring effective dimensionality.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Dimension dependence of spacing summaries.} Eigenvalue-spacing statistics depend on model dimension $p$ and on the estimated subspace (e.g., PCA choices). Throughout, we interpret minimum-gap and nearest-neighbor spacing summaries comparatively (between conditions and across subjects) under fixed pipeline settings, rather than as absolute measurements of latent neural singularities.

\paragraph{Spectral sensitivity.} Spectral sensitivity was defined as the within-subject Pearson correlation between spectral radius and minimum eigenvalue spacing across windows. All within-subject correlations reported autocorrelation-adjusted effective sample sizes ($n_\mathrm{eff}$) using Bartlett's formula: $n_\mathrm{eff} = n(1-\rho_x\rho_y)/(1+\rho_x\rho_y)$, where $\rho_x,\rho_y$ are lag-1 autocorrelations of the two series. $p$-values were recomputed at the adjusted degrees of freedom.

\paragraph{Cross-state comparability and subspace dependence.} For scalp EEG, the primary pipeline fitted PCA independently per condition/state. Because this changes the estimated subspace as well as coordinates, between-state differences in eigenvalue geometry are interpreted as descriptive summaries of best linear dynamics \emph{within each state-specific subspace}. To test whether the headline state effects were artifacts of state-specific coordinate systems, we additionally performed a shared-subspace PCA robustness analysis: within each subject, PCA was fit once on pooled CSD-transformed data across all states and each state was projected into the common subspace prior to VAR estimation. Both per-state and shared-subspace results are reported.

State-contrast analyses (task-relevant vs.\ task-irrelevant) used circular-shift permutation (5000 permutations) to preserve temporal autocorrelation structure.

\subsection{Chirality and eigenvalue-trajectory summaries}

For each subject, we tracked the two eigenvalues with the smallest gap across consecutive windows, forming a trajectory in the complex plane. From this trajectory we computed:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Chirality index}: the fraction of windows in which the eigenvalue pair exhibited complex splitting (i.e., nonzero imaginary-part difference).
    \item \textbf{Trajectory winding-style summary}: the cumulative angular change of the eigenvalue-pair difference vector across windows \citep{RafiUlIslam2024}. This is reported as a descriptive trajectory statistic and is not interpreted as a formally defined topological invariant of an underlying physical system.
    \item \textbf{Phase-like trajectory summary}: a geometric-phase-style summary accumulated along the trajectory. This quantity is used descriptively and is not taken as evidence for non-Hermitian topological structure.
    \item \textbf{Circular variance}: a measure of directional dispersion in the trajectory.
    \item \textbf{Asymmetry ratio}: the ratio of antisymmetric to total power in the decomposition $\mathbf{A}=(\mathbf{A}+\mathbf{A}^\top)/2+(\mathbf{A}-\mathbf{A}^\top)/2$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-frequency coupling}

Phase--amplitude coupling (PAC) was estimated between low-frequency phase carriers and the high-gamma amplitude envelope using the modulation index \citep{Daume2024,Sakakura2023}. PAC was correlated with criticality measures across subjects to test for selective coupling.

\subsection{Statistics}

Primary inferential statistics included paired $t$ tests with Cohen's $d$ effect sizes, Pearson and Spearman correlations, and linear mixed-effects models (with subject as a random intercept) for band comparisons.

\paragraph{Primary hypotheses and multiplicity.} To reduce analytic flexibility, we designate the following as primary endpoints: (H1) HG vs.\ BB difference in branching ratio $\sigma$; (H2) cross-subject association between $\sigma$ and EP score; (H3) state effect on minimum eigenvalue spacing in propofol (awake vs.\ sedated) and sleep (W vs.\ N3 vs.\ REM); (H4) near-zero within-state correlations between spacing and alpha (propofol) / delta (sleep). Other metrics (PAC, chirality-related observables, decoding reversal, and sensitivity) are treated as secondary/exploratory.

False-discovery-rate (FDR) correction \citep{BenjaminiHochberg1995} was applied to grouped families of tests within each analysis (e.g., all sleep pairwise state contrasts; all secondary correlations tested jointly). The significance threshold was $\alpha=0.05$ throughout, applied to FDR-corrected $p$-values where multiple tests were performed.

For the SVD dimension analysis, a random-matrix null model was used to control for mathematical coupling between EP score and effective rank: random matrices matching the empirical spectral radius distribution were generated and their rank--EP-score correlation served as the null distribution.

For the Petermann noise analysis, cross-correlation was computed using NaN-aware methods with non-overlapping stride (stride $=5$ windows) for the Granger-like precedence test, avoiding temporal leakage from overlapping windows.

\subsection{Robustness analyses}

Robustness checks were performed to evaluate identifiability, pipeline dependence, and cross-state comparability:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Surrogate controls (spectral sensitivity identifiability):} For 5 propofol subjects (subset: 1010, 1017, 1033, 1055, 1068), we generated 200 phase-randomized surrogates per subject. Phase randomization preserved the power spectrum and cross-channel coherence but destroyed fine temporal structure. VAR(1) fitting and spectral sensitivity computation were repeated on each surrogate; increasing surrogate count did not change the interpretive conclusion reported in Results.
    \item \textbf{Partial regression with alpha control:} The propofol change--change correlation (change in relative sensitivity vs.\ change in spacing) was recomputed as a partial correlation controlling for change in alpha power.
    \item \textbf{PCA dimensionality robustness:} The propofol pipeline was re-run with 10 and 20 PCA components (in addition to the standard 15).
    \item \textbf{Shared-subspace PCA:} Within each subject, PCA was fit once on pooled CSD-transformed data across all states and each state was projected into the common subspace prior to VAR estimation.
    \item \textbf{Alternative local-spacing metrics:} Nearest-neighbor spacing was summarized by the median and the 10th percentile in addition to the minimum gap.
    \item \textbf{Additional checks for seizure-aligned trajectory analyses (recommended):} explicitly test the timing of the ictal minimum (time-to-minimum relative to onset) and post-ictal recovery (post-ictal slope and/or post-ictal vs.\ ictal contrast); report both subject-averaged and seizure-level distributions to quantify heterogeneity; ensure the intended null/control analyses are implemented end-to-end (sham onsets in the main workflow; phase-randomized surrogates if not already applied within trajectory computation); verify the baseline window is sufficiently separated from onset to avoid normalizing out pre-ictal dynamics; and confirm plotting uses consistent units for seizure-aligned time.
    \item \textbf{Remaining robustness extensions (recommended):} window-length/overlap sweeps; alternative linear estimators (ridge-regularized VAR, state-space/subspace identification) to assess stability of $\rho$, spacing, and EP score.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, interpretation is anchored in (i) robustness to shared-subspace estimation (mitigating state-dependent PCA coordinate changes) and (ii) concordance across spacing summaries (minimum gap, median nearest-neighbor spacing, and low-quantile nearest-neighbor spacing). In contrast, the surrogate analysis indicates that absolute spectral sensitivity can arise from autoregressive fitting to autocorrelated signals, motivating comparative rather than standalone interpretation of sensitivity magnitude.
